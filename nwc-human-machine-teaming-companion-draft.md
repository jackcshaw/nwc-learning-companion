# Assuring Learning After Automation

## Teaching Frame Literacy as a Leadership Competency at NWC

AI in the classroom is often treated as a tool policy problem. Should students be allowed to use it? On which assignments? With what disclosure? Those questions matter, but they are downstream of the harder problem. The harder problem is assurance of learning. If AI can produce the artifact, what does the artifact still prove?

For a long time, the paper, brief, or analytic product served as an imperfect but useful proxy for the thinking behind it. The artifact never proved everything. That is why seminar discussion, faculty questioning, oral exams, iterative writing, and revision mattered. Still, a finished product usually carried some evidence that the student had performed the work. The student had gathered sources, formed a judgment, organized the argument, and made choices under constraint.

Generative AI weakens that relationship. A student can now produce a plausible artifact without doing the thinking the artifact is meant to represent. The visible failures are easy to name: generic prose, brittle claims, invented evidence, superficial synthesis, and arguments that collapse under questioning. The more dangerous failure is false learning. The answer is fluent, the student absorbs the model's frame, and the surface improves while ownership of the reasoning gets worse.

That is why the question is not merely "How should students use AI?" It is "How does a war college assure learning when AI can produce the visible signs of learning?" The answer cannot be a simple ban or a blanket permission. It has to be a shift in what the institution teaches, observes, and assesses.

The core claim of this essay is that AI commoditizes visible competence. It makes it cheap to produce summaries, outlines, first drafts, plausible counterarguments, and polished briefs. As that work becomes abundant, it becomes less reliable as evidence of learning. The scarce competency moves upstream. The student has to own the frame: the problem definition, assumptions, standards of evidence, theory of success, risk judgment, and interruption points that make the artifact meaningful.

This is not a new competency invented by AI. It is close to the heart of professional military education. What AI changes is the need to make that competency explicit.

The National War College already has a native vocabulary for this. Its [National Security Strategy Primer](https://nwc.ndu.edu/Portals/71/Documents/Publications/NWC-Primer-FINAL_for%20Web.pdf) presents strategy as disciplined practical judgment under uncertainty. Strategy is not a template. It is not a product that can be mechanically generated from a set of inputs. It begins with a problem frame. It requires clarity about interests, assumptions, ends, ways, means, costs, risks, tradeoffs, and the changing context in which policy and power operate. It also requires bias awareness, red-teaming, course correction, and reassessment.

That vocabulary matters because it keeps the AI question from becoming abstract. NWC does not need to import a foreign theory of "AI literacy" and bolt it onto the curriculum. It can start from its own account of strategic logic. The task is to teach students how to exercise that logic when AI is part of the decision stack.

The shift is subtle but important. AI does not remove the need for strategic judgment. It changes where judgment has to show up. When the model can generate the first version, the student's work is no longer proved by the existence of a first version. When the model can produce a counterargument, the student's work is not proved by the presence of a counterargument. When the model can generate options, the student's work is not proved by a list of options. The learning has to be visible in the student's relationship to the frame: what the model was asked to do, what it assumed, what it omitted, where it overreached, what standards were used to judge it, and why the final decision remained a human responsibility.

This is why "frame literacy" is a useful label, as long as it remains grounded in the work NWC already values. Frame literacy is the ability to see the frame being handed to an AI system through prompt, context, data, criteria, role, and institutional purpose; to revise that frame before trusting the output; to judge what the model did inside the frame; and to know when the frame itself has to change.

It is not a replacement for critical thinking. It is critical thinking in a setting where machines can produce many of the artifacts that used to reveal whether thinking had occurred.

This essay is also meant to model the method it recommends. The argument itself becomes the worked example: name the frame, test where it is useful or brittle, and then use the companion tool at the end to turn the essay into prompts, exercises, and assessment designs that instructors and students can practice.

## The Artifact No Longer Carries the Same Evidence

The educational problem begins with the artifact. A paper, memo, or briefing product used to compress a great deal of process. It showed what the student selected, ignored, emphasized, connected, and defended. Faculty could read the product and infer at least some of the learning underneath it. The inference was never perfect, but it was often good enough to support evaluation when paired with class participation and faculty knowledge of the student.

AI changes the evidentiary value of the artifact. It does not make writing meaningless. It does not make analysis impossible. It makes the visible product less reliable as a stand-in for the student's understanding.

That creates two different problems. The first is visible non-learning: the brief is thin, the reasoning collapses under questions, the prose carries the familiar smell of generated work, or the student cannot explain what the argument is doing. This failure is serious, but it is also useful because faculty and students can see it. The assessment has something to grab onto.

The second problem is harder: false learning. A student may use AI openly, follow the rules, produce a better artifact, and still fail to develop the intended judgment. The tool may supply the first useful frame. It may select the assumptions. It may decide what evidence matters. It may identify the policy tradeoffs. It may generate the language of balance, caution, and strategic sophistication. The student may then revise the prose without ever owning the deeper structure.

This is false learning. It is not simply that the student got away with something. It is that the student may sincerely believe learning occurred because the artifact improved. The institution may believe the same thing if the assessment is still organized around the artifact alone.

This risk is especially important in professional military education because the desired outcome is not fluent prose. The desired outcome is judgment. NWC graduates need to reason across domains, interests, institutions, adversaries, constraints, time horizons, and uncertainty. They need to work with incomplete evidence and contested values. They need to distinguish what is known, what is assumed, what is desired, and what is possible. They need to defend choices, not merely generate options.

AI can help with parts of that work. It can summarize, compare, generate alternatives, surface blind spots, role-play objections, and accelerate drafting. But if the model quietly supplies the underlying frame, the student may practice polish instead of judgment.

The assessment question therefore changes. The question is no longer simply, "Is this a good paper?" It becomes, "What does this paper show about the student's ownership of the reasoning?" That is a different standard. It demands different assignments, different review practices, and different evidence of learning.

## NWC Already Teaches the Competency AI Makes Urgent

The best reason to ground this argument in the NWC Primer is that it prevents the essay from sounding like a technology argument searching for an educational home. The Primer already describes a discipline of thought that resembles what AI now makes necessary.

It emphasizes that strategy begins with understanding the situation. That understanding is not passive. It requires framing the problem. What is happening? Why does it matter? Whose interests are implicated? What assumptions are being made? What are the desired ends? What ways and means might connect those ends to action? What risks and costs follow? What feedback would show that the strategy is failing?

Those are not prompt-engineering questions. They are strategic questions. But they become prompt-shaping questions once AI enters the workflow.

If a student asks an AI system to assess a regional crisis, the prompt necessarily contains a frame. It may be explicit or implicit. It may emphasize deterrence, alliance management, escalation control, domestic politics, institutional constraints, historical analogy, adversary intent, or force posture. The model will operate inside whatever frame it receives or infers. A fluent answer can hide that frame. It can make the response appear more objective than it is.

NWC's task is to teach students to see that frame. What problem is the model solving? What assumptions did it accept? What did it treat as fixed? What did it treat as variable? What kind of evidence did it privilege? What risks did it make visible? What risks did it bury? What would change the conclusion? Where should human judgment interrupt the workflow?

This is familiar territory. Faculty already press students on assumptions, scope, evidence, tradeoffs, feasibility, risk, and whether an argument survives challenge. AI does not change the value of that practice. It makes the practice more important because the model can hide weak reasoning under strong presentation.

One way to state the new educational objective is this: NWC should teach students to make the AI frame visible.

That means assignments should not only ask for a final product. They should ask students to identify the frame they used, the alternatives they considered, the model outputs they rejected, the assumptions they changed, and the evidence that would cause them to revise their conclusion. Faculty should be able to examine not just the answer, but the student's command of the frame that produced it.

This also means AI use should not be treated only as a disclosure problem. Disclosure matters, but disclosure alone does not prove learning. "I used AI to brainstorm" or "I used AI to edit" tells the instructor very little. The more important questions are: What did the AI assume? What did the student accept? What did the student reject? What did the student change? What did the student learn by challenging the model?

The answer to AI in the classroom is therefore not merely an AI policy. It is a pedagogy of visible judgment.

## Access Is Not Apprenticeship Into a Practice

The same distinction appears outside the classroom. Organizations often give capable people AI tools and let uses emerge. That produces activity. It does not necessarily produce shared capability. The license shows up immediately; the return depends on whether people learn the operating model around the tool.

That pattern matters for NWC because access to AI is not apprenticeship into AI-enabled judgment. Students can use the tool every day and still never learn the hidden parts of the practice: how experts define a problem, how they decide what evidence counts, how they notice a brittle assumption, how they recover from a bad frame, and how they remain accountable when a tool helped produce the work.

Cognitive apprenticeship is useful here because it describes how people learn skilled work that is partly tacit. They learn through modeling, coaching, articulation, reflection, and feedback. Instructors make expert moves visible, students try those moves under supervision, and the practice gradually becomes internal. AI does not remove the need for that kind of apprenticeship. If anything, it increases it, because more of the visible artifact can be produced without the student ever seeing the expert move underneath.

The goal, then, is not simply to give students AI access or teach them a set of prompts. The goal is to apprentice them into the human side of the human-AI-human loop. A human sets the frame, AI collapses work inside that frame, and a human judges, revises, or extends the result. In the classroom, the point is not to keep students away from the middle of that loop. It is to make them competent at both human ends of it.

## What AI Makes Cheap

Sarah Guo's ["The Untrainable"](https://saranormous.substack.com/p/the-untrainable) is useful because it sharpens the economic and institutional pattern behind this educational problem. The basic idea is that AI absorbs work that can be made visible, measured, repeated, and evaluated. Once a capability is captured by the system, it becomes cheaper and more broadly available. The scarce value moves elsewhere.

In business, the remaining value often lives where correctness is private, contextual, permissioned, or accountable. The model can generate options, but it does not know what is really true inside the organization unless that truth has been made available. It can optimize toward a metric, but it does not decide which metric should matter. It can produce competent-looking work, but it does not become accountable for the consequences.

The same pattern applies to education. AI makes visible competence cheap. Summaries become cheap. Outlines become cheap. Plausible historical analogies become cheap. Balanced paragraphs become cheap. Lists of policy options become cheap. Even decent counterarguments become cheap.

That does not mean those things are worthless. It means they no longer prove what they used to prove. If a student produces a polished summary, the summary itself may not show that the student has learned how to summarize. If the student produces three courses of action, the list itself may not show that the student understands strategic choice. If the student produces a balanced essay, the balance may reflect the model's rhetorical habits rather than the student's judgment.

The educational value moves to the parts of the work that cannot be reduced to generic visible competence. What is the real problem? What standard of success matters? Which assumptions are dangerous? What would the adversary exploit? What does the institution know that the model does not? Which friction in the work is developmental? What tradeoff is being avoided by elegant language? Who is accountable for the recommendation?

Those questions are not easy to automate because they depend on purpose, context, and responsibility. They are also exactly the kinds of questions NWC exists to teach.

This is where the "AI makes judgment disappear" story is wrong. AI does not make judgment disappear. It makes judgment easier to hide. The student can hide behind the artifact. The institution can hide behind a policy. The model can hide the frame inside fluent language. The task of instruction is to bring judgment back into view.

## The Jagged Frontier and Appropriate Reliance

The work by Fabrizio Dell'Acqua, Ethan Mollick, and their coauthors on the [jagged technological frontier](https://www.hbs.edu/faculty/Pages/item.aspx?num=64700) gives this argument an empirical backbone. In their study of knowledge workers, AI improved performance on some tasks and degraded performance on others. The frontier was not obvious. Tasks that looked similar from the outside could differ in whether AI helped or hurt.

That is exactly the condition future leaders will face. AI will be useful enough to invite reliance and uneven enough to make reliance dangerous. The problem will not be solved by telling students that AI is powerful. It will not be solved by telling them that AI is risky. Both are true. The educational target is frontier awareness: knowing what kind of task one is in, what the model is likely to do well, where it may fail, and what human checks are required.

This is where Raees and Papangelis become especially useful. Their work on ["From Trust to Appropriate Reliance"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.23896) argues that trust is not the right endpoint. A user may trust too much, trust too little, or trust in the wrong way. The more precise target is appropriate reliance: relying on AI when it is warranted, withholding reliance when it is not, and calibrating the relationship to the task, evidence, and consequences.

That language fits leadership education better than generic "AI literacy." Future officers do not need to be trained simply to trust or distrust AI. They need to learn when to rely, when to verify, when to interrupt, when to redirect, and when to refuse the frame altogether.

Their related review, ["From Explainable to Interactive AI"](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.15051), adds another important move. It argues that human-AI interaction should not stop at explanation or contestability. Users need agency to adapt, shape, and even co-design systems. That matters for the companion layer of this project. If students only inspect an AI answer after it is produced, they remain downstream of the frame. If they learn to shape the frame, change the interaction, and revise the system's working context, they become active participants in the reasoning process.

This distinction should matter to NWC. A future leader who can ask an AI system for an answer is not yet exercising judgment. A future leader who can shape the system's task, identify its assumptions, set evaluation criteria, test outputs, and decide when to interrupt is much closer to the competency the institution should care about.

Mollick and Mollick's work on [Assigning AI](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.10052) and [Instructors as Innovators](https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.05181) points in the same direction from the instructional side. AI can function as tutor, coach, simulator, teammate, critic, or student, but those roles have to be designed. The learning value does not come from the mere presence of the tool. It comes from the structure of the interaction.

That suggests a practical rule for NWC: do not ask whether AI should be "allowed" in the abstract. Ask what role the AI is playing in the learning loop. Is it generating? Critiquing? Role-playing? Stress-testing? Translating? Simulating an adversary? Acting as a student whose work must be corrected? Each role creates different evidence of learning.

The same tool can either weaken or strengthen learning depending on the frame. If AI supplies the first plausible answer and the student passively accepts it, the tool may undermine judgment. If AI produces a flawed assessment that the student must diagnose and defend against questioning, the tool may strengthen judgment. The difference is not the tool. The difference is the instructional frame.

## A Ladder of Framing Responsibility

The integration question becomes clearer if AI use is treated as a ladder of framing responsibility rather than a binary choice. The aim is not to climb as high as possible for every task. The aim is to know which rung a student or instructor is standing on, what frame is being inherited, and what kind of judgment the assignment is meant to develop.

At the bottom is generic AI use. A student treats a model like a smarter search box: little context, no explicit purpose, and no standard for a good output. This may be useful for quick orientation, but it teaches very little by itself. The student inherits the model's frame almost completely.

The next rung is structured prompting and context. The student tells the model what problem is being worked, what material matters, what role the model should play, and how the answer should be judged. This is better, but it can still leave the student downstream of the tool if the criteria are borrowed or shallow.

Above that is a reusable workflow or agent skill. The work has explicit steps, tools, review criteria, stopping points, and audit trails. This rung begins to look more like supervised professional practice because the student has to understand the process, not merely the prompt.

The next rung is an evaluator loop or agent team. Multiple perspectives test the same frame. Disagreement becomes visible. A red-team agent, source-checking agent, or adversary-role agent can help students see how the initial frame might fail. Here the educational value comes from the student's ability to interpret disagreement, not from the mere existence of multiple outputs.

At the top is an institution-shaped workflow or model built on reviewed material, local standards, and faculty judgment. This is powerful because it can carry NWC's way of reasoning beyond the seminar. It is also risky because it can reproduce a single frame at scale if its assumptions are not visible.

This ladder does two jobs. In the classroom, it gives faculty a way to decide what kind of AI use is appropriate for a learning objective. Outside the classroom, it shows how NWC's frames could become institutional assets: prompts, rubrics, workflows, evaluator loops, and eventually more tailored systems that help planners practice a disciplined way of reasoning.

The central caution is that exported frames must carry their assumptions on their face. Otherwise a tool meant to spread judgment can spread uniformity. Doctrine is at least argued over in the open. A silent workflow can shape a generation of planners without anyone noticing the frame it has normalized.

## The Frame Is Not the Framer

One objection to this argument is that stronger AI systems may eventually make frame literacy unnecessary. If today's systems can generate drafts, tomorrow's systems may generate better problem definitions, better assumptions, and better strategies. They may compare frames, challenge the user's frame, and keep searching for stronger approaches. If the model can reframe its own work, perhaps the human role has simply moved out of the way.

The problem with that objection is that it moves responsibility up one level. It does not remove it. A system can operate inside a frame, compare frames, or reframe in pursuit of a goal. But it is still optimizing toward some purpose, signal, reward, constraint, or instruction that someone supplied, accepted, or allowed to govern the work. Someone still decides what should count as progress.

That is the distinction NWC should teach. AI may help formulate alternatives. It may reveal inconsistencies. It may propose assumptions. It may even challenge the user's initial framing. But the decision about what matters, what risks are acceptable, what tradeoffs are legitimate, and what purposes deserve pursuit cannot be outsourced without outsourcing responsibility itself.

This is especially true in national security work. Strategy is not a puzzle with a single answer waiting to be found. It involves contested interests, adversary adaptation, institutional politics, incomplete information, moral constraint, and consequences that cannot be fully known in advance. A model can assist the work, but it cannot become the accountable strategic actor.

This does not mean humans are always wiser than machines. Humans are biased, inattentive, overconfident, politically constrained, and prone to motivated reasoning. That is why red-teaming, structured analytic techniques, seminar challenge, and institutional review matter. The point is not human infallibility. The point is human responsibility.

Frame literacy is therefore not a romantic defense of unaided human judgment. It is a discipline for using machine capability without losing contact with purpose, evidence, uncertainty, and accountability.

## The Developmental Role of Friction

Greg Ceccarelli's ["Apprenticeship Was the Point"](https://www.meditationsontech.com/p/apprenticeship-was-the-point) gives the essay a crucial caution. Some work looks inefficient because it is waste. Some work looks inefficient because it is how judgment forms.

That distinction matters because AI is very good at removing friction. It can remove the blank page. It can remove the first ugly outline. It can remove the struggle to synthesize sources. It can remove the embarrassment of an unfinished thought. It can remove the time spent finding language for a half-formed idea.

Some of that is good. Not every friction deserves preservation. Formatting friction, clerical friction, search friction, and repetitive drafting friction can waste time. AI can free students and faculty to spend more attention on judgment.

But some friction is load-bearing. The struggle to define the problem may be where learning happens. The first failed attempt to connect ends, ways, and means may be how a student discovers that the argument is incoherent. The discomfort of defending a claim in seminar may be how confidence becomes calibrated. Revision may matter not because the final sentence improves, but because the student sees how the argument changed.

If AI removes that friction too early, it can create a more polished artifact and a weaker learner.

The practical question is not "Should students struggle?" It is "Which struggles are developmental?" NWC should not protect difficulty for its own sake. It should identify the forms of effort that build strategic judgment and design AI use around them.

For example, AI may be very useful after a student has written an initial problem frame. The model can test the frame, suggest missing assumptions, generate alternative interpretations, and simulate objections. But if AI supplies the first frame before the student has struggled to form one, the student may never develop the underlying muscle.

Similarly, AI may be useful after a student has created a draft strategy. It can identify risks, point to unstated assumptions, or role-play an adversary. But if the model generates the strategy and the student merely edits the language, the learning evidence is thin.

The lesson is not that AI should be kept out of early work in every case. Rather, instructors should decide where the developmental friction sits. They should preserve the friction that builds judgment and automate the friction that merely consumes time.

That requires faculty judgment. It also requires explicit design. If instructors do not decide which friction matters, the tool will decide by default.

## From Tacit Judgment to Traceable Artifacts

Ceccarelli's ["The Refinery"](https://www.meditationsontech.com/p/the-refinery) helps with the institutional side of the argument. Durable institutions do not merely possess tacit judgment. They convert it into forms that can be taught, inspected, revised, and reused. Rubrics, evals, correction logs, after-action reviews, doctrine, workflows, and review criteria are all ways of refining judgment into durable artifacts.

NWC already does this in many forms. It exports judgment through graduates, faculty scholarship, seminar practice, exercises, wargames, doctrine-adjacent work, and professional networks. AI creates a new opportunity and a new risk. It becomes possible to capture parts of NWC's reasoning practice in prompts, agents, evaluation rubrics, simulations, and interactive teaching tools. That could extend the institution's reach. It could also flatten its judgment into a brittle template if done carelessly.

The difference lies in traceability. An AI-enabled teaching workflow should not simply produce answers in the style of NWC. It should make the reasoning structure visible. It should show the assumptions, criteria, failure modes, sources of uncertainty, review process, and points where human judgment must enter.

This is where DoD responsible AI principles are relevant. Responsible, traceable, reliable, and governable AI should not be abstract compliance language. In an educational setting, those principles can become learning design criteria. Can the student trace how the output was produced? Can the student explain which parts were accepted and rejected? Can the instructor see whether the student relied appropriately? Can the workflow be interrupted, reviewed, and corrected?

Higher-education assessment frameworks, including AI assessment scales, point in the same direction. The institution needs more than a binary rule about AI use. It needs levels of allowed use, clear learning outcomes, and assessment forms that match those outcomes. If the learning outcome is writing fluency, the assessment should look different than if the outcome is strategic framing, adversarial critique, or judgment under uncertainty.

For NWC, the goal should not be to make every assignment "AI-proof." That is probably impossible and may be undesirable. The goal should be to make learning visible in a world where AI is present.

That means students may be required to submit process artifacts: initial frames, prompt logs, rejected outputs, assumption audits, model critique notes, revised criteria, and reflection on appropriate reliance. Those artifacts are not bureaucratic add-ons if designed well. They are evidence of learning.

Faculty can also create AI-generated artifacts for students to critique. A polished but flawed strategic assessment may be more pedagogically useful than a blank-page essay prompt. The student must identify the hidden frame, assumptions, missing evidence, bias, risk treatment, and failure point. Then the student must defend that critique orally. In that design, AI is not a shortcut around learning. It becomes the object of disciplined judgment.

## Assessment After Automation

If the artifact no longer proves what it used to prove, assessment has to change. The central assessment question should be: Can the student demonstrate ownership of the frame?

That can be assessed in several ways.

First, assignments can require a frame statement. Before submitting the final product, the student must state the problem frame, key assumptions, criteria for success, evidence standard, anticipated failure modes, and the role AI played in the work. This does not need to be long. It needs to be specific.

Second, assignments can require an assumption audit. Students should identify the assumptions embedded in both their own reasoning and any AI outputs they used. They should distinguish assumptions that are explicit, inferred, inherited from the model, or introduced by the assignment itself.

Third, assignments can require output triage. Students should show which AI outputs they accepted, modified, rejected, or investigated further. The point is not to punish AI use. The point is to see whether the student exercised judgment over the output.

Fourth, faculty can use oral defense. A short oral defense can reveal quickly whether the student owns the reasoning. What did the model miss? What would change your conclusion? Why did you accept that assumption? What alternative frame did you consider? Where would you not rely on the model? What risk is hidden by your chosen language?

Fifth, faculty can assign flawed AI assessments. The student receives a fluent answer that is wrong at the level of frame, not merely wrong on facts. The task is to diagnose the hidden frame, expose the assumptions, identify what evidence is missing, and revise the approach. This is likely one of the highest-value pilot designs because it turns the AI risk into an assessment instrument.

Sixth, students can be asked to build a reusable artifact from their critique. That artifact might be a rubric, checklist, prompt, evaluation guide, red-team protocol, or after-action note. This connects individual learning to institutional learning. The student's work does not end with a paper. It becomes a small contribution to the institution's capacity to teach and govern AI-enabled reasoning.

The most important feature across all of these designs is that they move assessment from product alone to product plus ownership. The final artifact still matters. NWC should not abandon clarity, rigor, evidence, or writing quality. But those qualities should be understood as outputs of a reasoning process, not substitutes for it.

## Failure Modes Worth Teaching Against

The long-form argument should name the failure modes directly, because they are the practical reason faculty and students need a new method. The danger is not only that students will use AI too much. It is that they will use it in ways that look productive while weakening the very competencies NWC wants to build.

The first failure mode is frame capture. The model supplies the first plausible frame and the student never recovers enough distance to challenge it. This is especially dangerous because the frame may be reasonable. It may not contain any obvious hallucination. It may simply define the problem too narrowly, privilege one set of interests, assume a theory of adversary behavior, or treat a constraint as fixed when it should be contested. Once the frame is accepted, later revisions improve the answer inside the wrong boundary.

The second failure mode is fluency substitution. The student mistakes well-ordered language for well-owned reasoning. AI is good at producing the tone of analytic maturity: balanced paragraphs, caveats, transitions, and a measured voice. Those features can conceal the absence of hard choices. In strategy, fluency can become a way to avoid deciding. A paragraph that "balances" every consideration may never identify which risk matters most.

The third failure mode is premature synthesis. Students may ask AI to synthesize before they have done enough work to know what should be synthesized. The output may connect sources, themes, and concepts in a way that feels coherent. But if the student cannot reconstruct why those connections matter, the synthesis belongs to the model. The student has skipped the developmental struggle of forming a mental map.

The fourth failure mode is uncalibrated reliance. A student may rely on AI because it is usually useful, because the output is confident, or because the task feels tedious. The problem is that AI performance is uneven. The jagged frontier means that the system may be strong at one part of the workflow and weak at another. Appropriate reliance requires the student to identify which part of the task is being delegated or supported, what evidence would justify reliance, and what independent checks are required.

The fifth failure mode is invisible delegation. The student may not realize which parts of the work have been delegated. Asking for "feedback" may delegate criteria. Asking for "a better structure" may delegate the argument. Asking for "counterarguments" may delegate the range of imaginable objections. Asking for "a more strategic version" may delegate the meaning of strategic. The language of assistance can hide the transfer of judgment.

The sixth failure mode is institutional monoculture. If many students use similar tools, prompts, examples, and model defaults, the range of frames may narrow. AI can create a surface appearance of diversity while reproducing common assumptions. In a war college, that risk matters. Strategic education should widen the student's ability to see competing frames, not compress the class toward a model's average answer.

The seventh failure mode is responsibility laundering. A recommendation may become easier to defend because the model generated it, or easier to soften because the model's language distributes agency. But AI does not become accountable for the recommendation. The human remains responsible for the final judgment. Students need to practice saying, "I accepted this output because..." and "I rejected this output because..." and "I remain accountable for this conclusion despite using the system."

Each failure mode suggests an assessment move. Frame capture can be tested by asking students to produce alternative frames. Fluency substitution can be tested through oral defense. Premature synthesis can be tested by requiring intermediate artifacts. Uncalibrated reliance can be tested through reliance logs and verification plans. Invisible delegation can be tested by asking students to identify what role the AI played at each stage. Monoculture can be tested through red-team comparison across student outputs. Responsibility laundering can be tested by forcing students to defend decisions in first-person terms.

This is why the solution is not an AI detector. The detector asks whether AI was used. The more important question is what AI use did to the student's relationship with the reasoning.

## What Students Should Have to Show

If frame ownership is the desired competency, NWC needs a clear account of what evidence would show it. Otherwise "frame literacy" risks becoming another attractive phrase that floats above instruction.

A student who owns the frame should be able to do at least six things.

First, the student should be able to state the problem in a way that distinguishes condition from problem. Not every undesirable condition is the problem to be solved. A region may be unstable, an alliance may be strained, a competitor may be active, or a capability gap may exist. But the strategic problem depends on interests, stakes, timing, actors, and feasible choices. AI can describe the condition. The student must decide what problem matters for strategy.

Second, the student should be able to identify assumptions. Some assumptions will be about adversary intent, partner behavior, domestic constraints, institutional capacity, time horizons, escalation dynamics, or the meaning of success. Students should distinguish between assumptions they made, assumptions inherited from the model, and assumptions embedded in the assignment or source material. The Primer's emphasis on assumptions gives NWC a strong basis for making this explicit.

Third, the student should be able to define an evidence standard. What kind of evidence would strengthen the argument? What evidence would weaken it? Which claims are supported, inferred, speculative, or value-laden? What is the confidence level? What uncertainty remains? AI can generate evidence-like prose, but the student must be able to judge evidentiary weight.

Fourth, the student should be able to explain reliance decisions. Where did AI help? Where did it not help? What output was accepted, rejected, or modified? What was verified independently? Where was the tool not used because the task required unaided judgment, sensitive context, or developmental struggle? This is where Raees and Papangelis are especially useful. Appropriate reliance is not a mood. It is a practice that can be described and assessed.

Fifth, the student should be able to produce alternative frames. Strategic judgment improves when students can see that the first frame is not inevitable. A crisis can be framed as deterrence failure, alliance management, domestic legitimacy, resource allocation, technological surprise, or institutional coordination. Different frames reveal different ends, ways, means, risks, and tradeoffs. AI can help generate alternatives, but the student must judge which are useful and why.

Sixth, the student should be able to defend the final judgment under questioning. This may be the most important evidence of ownership. A student who owns the frame can respond when pressed. The answer may change under challenge, but the student can explain why. A student who does not own the frame often returns to the language of the artifact. The prose remains polished, but the reasoning has no depth behind it.

These six capabilities could become the basis for a frame ownership rubric. The rubric would not replace faculty judgment. It would give faculty and students a shared language for what the institution is trying to observe.

For example, a weak submission might disclose AI use but show no ability to explain what the model assumed. A stronger submission might identify model assumptions but accept the original problem frame uncritically. A still stronger submission might compare multiple frames, justify reliance decisions, and defend why one frame was chosen. The strongest submission would show that AI expanded the student's reasoning without displacing ownership of the decision.

This approach also makes AI use less mysterious. Students would know that the question is not whether they can hide the tool or decorate the final product. The question is whether they can show disciplined command of the reasoning process in which the tool participated.

## Faculty Practice and Institutional Governance

This shift would ask more of faculty, not less. That is unavoidable. AI does not reduce the need for expert teaching. It increases the need for faculty to make expert judgment more visible.

Faculty do not need to become technologists in the narrow sense. They do not need to master every model, tool, or prompt pattern. They do need enough working fluency to understand how AI changes the evidence of learning. They need to know when a polished artifact is thin evidence, how to ask questions that reveal ownership, and how to design assignments that make reasoning visible.

A practical faculty development sequence could begin with the same exercise students will experience. Faculty review a polished AI-generated strategic assessment and identify the hidden frame, assumptions, missing evidence, and failure point. Then they compare how different instructors diagnosed the same answer. That comparison would be valuable because it surfaces tacit teaching judgment. It shows what faculty notice, what they reward, and what they consider dangerous.

From there, faculty could build a small shared library of flawed AI assessments. Each artifact would include the student-facing output, the instructor key, likely student traps, oral defense questions, and rubric criteria. Over time, this library would become part of NWC's institutional refinery: a way to turn tacit judgment into reusable teaching material.

Governance matters because AI-enabled teaching artifacts can travel. A prompt, rubric, agent workflow, or flawed-assessment library can shape how students reason. That creates value and risk. The value is that strong teaching judgment becomes shareable. The risk is that one frame becomes too dominant, invisible assumptions get embedded into the workflow, or faculty begin to outsource their own judgment to a tool designed to support it.

The governance standard should therefore be modest but real. AI-enabled instructional tools should document their purpose, assumptions, intended users, data boundaries, review process, failure modes, and human decision points. Faculty should know what the tool is meant to do and what it is not meant to do. Students should know which parts of the work remain theirs.

This is another place where DoD responsible AI principles become useful for education. Traceability, reliability, and governability can be translated into classroom practice. Traceability means the student can reconstruct the reasoning process. Reliability means the workflow is tested against known failure modes. Governability means the human can interrupt, revise, or reject the system's contribution.

In other words, the classroom can become a place where responsible AI is not merely discussed, but practiced.

## The Pilot

The first pilot should be small. NWC does not need to solve AI across the curriculum in one move. It should choose one existing assignment or analytic task where framing matters.

One possible pilot would work like this.

Students first produce a short problem frame without AI. They identify the strategic problem, key assumptions, relevant actors, desired ends, possible ways and means, risks, and evidence needed. This preserves the developmental friction of initial framing.

Students then use AI to challenge the frame. The model is asked to identify assumptions, generate alternative frames, role-play a skeptical faculty member, and surface risks or blind spots. Students must record what they accepted, rejected, and changed.

Next, students receive or generate a polished but flawed AI assessment. The flaw should be at the level of frame. Perhaps it optimizes for short-term stability while ignoring alliance credibility. Perhaps it treats military feasibility as the primary constraint while burying domestic political risk. Perhaps it assumes adversary rationality too quickly. Perhaps it uses historical analogy in a way that makes the present case look easier than it is.

Students diagnose the flawed assessment. They identify the hidden frame, assumptions, missing evidence, and failure point. They revise the frame and produce a short final recommendation.

Finally, students complete a short oral defense. Faculty ask questions that test frame ownership: What did the AI get wrong? What did it make easier? Where did you rely on it appropriately? Where did you refuse it? What changed between your first and final frame? What evidence would change your recommendation?

The pilot should be evaluated not by whether students liked the tool, but by whether faculty could see more clearly who owned the reasoning. Did the process reveal false learning? Did it improve seminar discussion? Did it help students articulate assumptions? Did it create better evidence for assessment? Did it preserve the developmental friction that matters?

If the pilot works, NWC can refine it into a repeatable teaching pattern. It can create rubrics for frame ownership, appropriate reliance, assumption auditing, and oral defense. It can build a library of flawed AI assessments. It can capture common failure modes. It can train faculty to use AI outputs as objects of critique rather than as threats to be policed from outside the learning process.

## The Larger Institutional Opportunity

The long-term opportunity is not merely better classroom policy. It is an institutional method for human-machine teaming in professional military education.

NWC could become explicit about the competencies future leaders need when AI is part of the decision environment:

1. Frame the problem before asking the system to solve it.
2. State assumptions and standards of evidence.
3. Use AI to expand, test, and challenge the frame.
4. Calibrate reliance to the task and risk.
5. Preserve developmental friction where judgment forms.
6. Trace accepted and rejected outputs.
7. Red-team machine reasoning.
8. Defend the final judgment under questioning.
9. Convert lessons into reusable institutional artifacts.

This is more than prompt engineering. Prompt engineering is too narrow a label. The deeper competency is leadership over reasoning systems.

That phrase may sound abstract, but it is practical. A leader using AI-enabled staff tools will need to know what the system is optimizing for, what data it can see, what assumptions are embedded in its outputs, what it cannot know, what risks are hidden by confidence, and when to intervene. A leader will also need to decide what should not be delegated, what must remain accountable, and what kind of human review is required before action.

Those are educational questions before they are operational questions. If students do not practice them in the classroom, they will encounter them later in settings with higher stakes and less time.

The classroom is therefore a safer place to build the habit. Faculty can create controlled failures. They can let students experience the seductive fluency of a bad answer. They can teach students to slow down, expose the frame, and defend a judgment. They can show that the goal is not to beat the machine or obey it. The goal is to use it as part of disciplined reasoning.

The companion agentic tool proposed below is one way to make that possible.

## Why This Belongs at NWC

There is a temptation to treat AI as a general university problem, and in some respects it is. Every school is dealing with authorship, assessment, student use, faculty fluency, and institutional policy. But NWC has a more specific reason to take the problem seriously. The institution prepares leaders for environments where human-machine systems will increasingly shape staff work, intelligence analysis, planning, logistics, communication, and decision support. The classroom is not separate from that future operating environment. It is a place to rehearse it.

That rehearsal should not be framed as technical training alone. Future leaders do not need to become model engineers in order to lead in AI-enabled environments. They need to understand enough about the systems to ask better questions, assign responsibility, interpret outputs, recognize failure modes, and preserve human accountability. They need to know when an apparently efficient workflow has removed the friction that would have built judgment. They need to know when a model's answer is useful but the model's frame is wrong.

This is why the educational response should be ambitious but practical. NWC can teach AI-enabled strategic reasoning without turning every course into a technology course. It can use existing seminar practices, oral defense, red-teaming, wargaming, writing, and strategy formulation as the foundation. The change is to make the human-AI-human loop explicit: human framing, machine assistance, human judgment, and institutional learning.

If NWC does this well, it will not merely adapt to AI. It will help define what responsible AI-enabled leadership looks like in professional military education.

## Conclusion

AI forces a change in assurance of learning because it changes what artifacts can prove. A polished product is no longer enough evidence that a student owns the reasoning behind it. That is not a reason to panic. It is a reason to teach more precisely.

NWC already teaches the underlying competency. Strategic logic depends on framing, assumptions, evidence, risk, red-teaming, and reassessment. AI makes those competencies more visible and more urgent. It also creates an opportunity to assess them more directly.

The future educational question is not whether students can produce work with AI. They can. The question is whether they can remain responsible for the frame when AI is part of the work.

The aim should be to graduate leaders who can use AI without becoming downstream of it: leaders who can set purpose, shape context, calibrate reliance, contest outputs, preserve developmental friction, and defend judgment under pressure.

AI will make many forms of visible competence cheap. NWC's task is to teach the judgment that decides what competence should mean.

# Companion Agentic Tool

## Agent Mode

The companion should sit behind the essay as an agentic layer. The essay makes the argument. The companion helps a reader practice it. Its purpose is not to summarize the essay or generate a generic study guide. Its purpose is to operationalize frame literacy for instructors, students, and curriculum designers.

Agent Mode should work like a launcher. A reader copies the setup prompt, points Codex, Claude Code, OpenClaw, or another coding agent at the companion repo, and asks it to use the source kit before answering. That source kit keeps the essay, claims, prompts, objections, sources, transfer case, and traceable learning artifact in one place.

The first task is always the essay itself. The agent should help the reader identify the essay's frame, claims, assumptions, objections, and teaching implications. Only after that should it transfer the method to an approved NWC-style artifact.

## Source Kit

- `README.md` explains the purpose, setup prompt, and public-safe boundary.
- `AGENTS.md` gives the agent operating rules.
- `claims.md` maps the core claims, evidence, unresolved questions, and where to look next.
- `prompts/starter-prompts.md` provides copyable prompts for understanding, evidence inspection, exercise design, flawed assessments, oral defense, and trace artifacts.
- `prompts/objections-and-responses.md` gives strong objections and practical tests.
- `sources/source-spine.md` separates the public source spine from the essay's interpretation.
- `cases/cyber-group-strategy-transfer-case.md` describes the transfer exercise without publishing local course material.
- `artifacts/traceable-learning-artifact.md` gives the inspectable output faculty can review.

## Core Activities

1. Understand the argument.
2. Inspect the claims.
3. Design an NWC exercise.
4. Create a flawed AI assessment.
5. Run oral defense.
6. Build the traceable learning artifact.

## Public-Safe Boundary

The public repo should include the essay, prompts, claim map, source notes, and exercise scaffolds. It should not include private notes, local course artifacts, or anything not explicitly cleared for public use.
