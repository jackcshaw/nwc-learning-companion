# The Irreducible Officer — "Whose Turf?" Review

**Date:** 2026-06-28
**Scope:** Only the passages where the essay steps from AI authority (Jack's lane) onto military / command / PME-doctrine authority (Andres's lane). Everything else in the draft is in-lane and not flagged here.

---

## Why this matters

The whole opening was deliberately re-anchored to AI authority: Jack diagnoses what AI does to a work product; the command and operational consequences belong to Andres and the faculty. The primary reader is Richard Andres — a National War College professor who wrote the adjacent command-accountability piece and runs NWC's AI program.

Against that reader, two failure modes are expensive:

1. **Claiming authority in an area we don't hold** — asserting command doctrine or PME-internal fact as if from inside the profession.
2. **Sounding fluent where we don't know the right words** — narrating the lived texture of command, or generating strategic content, in a way that exposes a non-practitioner.

Both undercut the essay's credibility precisely with the person whose judgment decides whether it's high-value. The fix in every case below is to **retreat to the AI claim or generalize the example** — *not* to find better military wording. Better military wording would just substitute one set of guesses for another in the place where precision matters most. The operational instantiation is Andres's to supply (e.g., during his review), not ours to perform.

---

## The real strays (fix these)

### 1. Line 75 — the "presence" paragraph (Section IV). Highest risk.

> "…synthesizing a quality of signal that a model cannot be accountable for: the discomfort visible in **a staff officer's delivery**, the **overheard remark that reframes an adversary's stated position**, the institutional constraint that exists only as unwritten practice, the intuition that something about the official framing is wrong."

**Why it strays:** The underlying claim is solid AI-lane — a model can't access tacit signal or answer for it. But two of the four illustrations claim the *felt experience of command*: reading a staff officer's delivery, the overheard remark about an adversary. That is the texture of Andres's world, narrated as if we live in it. The other two images (unwritten institutional practice, the official framing feeling wrong) are general and fine.

**Pull-back:** Keep the claim. Generalize or cut the two command-specific images so they're tacit signals anyone in any organization would recognize, or state the point plainly and leave the command instantiation out. Do not replace them with "more authentic" military vignettes.

### 2. Line 117 — command doctrine asserted directly (Section VII).

> "…where strategy runs through contested interests, adversary adaptation, institutional politics, incomplete information, and moral constraint. AI can accelerate staff work. **Command responsibility cannot be transferred.**"

**Why it strays:** "Command responsibility cannot be transferred" is *Andres's own thesis*, stated as our doctrine — it reads as explaining his field back to him. It isn't wrong; it's his to assert, not ours. Separately, "strategy runs through [five things]" is us characterizing the nature of strategy — defensible, but it's the most asserted, least-owned line in the section.

**Pull-back:** Let the Andres citation two sentences later carry the command-responsibility point (attribute it rather than declare it). Re-aim the "strategy runs through…" line toward *why the AI problem bites here* (our angle) rather than a definition of strategy.

### 3. Line 121 — how a commander evaluates (Section VII).

> "A commander can weigh the confidence of the person making the recommendation against experience, incentives, reputation, and the way they answer under pressure."

**Why it strays:** This narrates the commander's evaluative process — his turf. It's in service of a legitimate AI point (a human recommendation carries signals AI lacks), but it makes that point by describing how command judgment works.

**Pull-back:** Keep the AI contrast (the human signals AI can't carry); drop the description of how a commander reads them.

## Accuracy checks against material we control (verify, don't assert)

### 4. Line 153 — the Primer enumeration (Section IX).

> "…the problem frame first, then interests, assumptions, ends, ways, means, costs, risks, and reassessment."

**Why it matters:** We're reproducing the structure of an actual NWC document (*A National Security Strategy Primer*) to NWC readers. If the elements or their order are off, Andres clocks it instantly. Not a lane problem — an accuracy one.

**Action:** Check the list and its order against the Primer directly.

### 5. Line 161 — strategic content we generate (pilot, step three).

> "…perhaps it optimizes for short-term stability while ignoring alliance credibility; it may treat military feasibility as the primary constraint while burying domestic political risk; it may assume adversary rationality…"

**Why it matters:** Lower risk, because the "perhaps / may" framing presents these as a *hypothetical faculty-built* flawed assessment rather than our analysis. But it's the one place in the pilot where we generate strategic failure-modes, so it's where unfluent framing would surface.

**Action:** Confirm these read as plausible strategic errors, or lean harder on the "this is the kind of flaw faculty would design in" attribution so the specificity belongs to the exercise, not to us.

## Bottom line

The three real strays (#1, #2, #3) all live in Sections IV and VII, and all resolve the same way: retreat to the AI claim and let the citation carry the command doctrine. #4 and #5 are accuracy checks against material we control. Nothing here requires military expertise to fix — only the discipline to stop one step short of it.
